1. Executive Summary & User Flows
1.1 Product Thesis
SpareKG is a two-sided marketplace that monetizes a structurally underutilized resource: the unused checked-baggage allowance of international travelers. Two clear pain points anchor the value proposition. Travelers fly with 10-20 kg of unused allowance on every trip, which has zero salvage value to the airline and the passenger. Senders, meanwhile, face courier rates of USD 8-15 per kilogram on the Kuala Lumpur to Dhaka corridor with delivery windows of 5-10 business days, often saddled with customs friction on items that would be uncontroversial in personal luggage.
The platform sits between these two parties as a trust, discovery, and settlement layer. It does not move physical goods, hold inventory, or operate as a freight forwarder. Its role is to verify identities, surface relevant flights, mediate price discovery through a points-based interaction economy, and establish the rails for safe handoff and dispute resolution.
1.2 Target Users & Initial Market
The launch corridor is Kuala Lumpur to Dhaka, chosen for three structural reasons. First, the route has high volume of Bangladeshi diaspora travel, supplying a steady inventory of trusted travelers. Second, the price gap between airline excess-baggage fees and informal courier rates is wide enough to support meaningful margins on both sides. Third, the founding team has direct cultural and language access to both endpoints, which is critical for KYC verification and dispute resolution in the early growth phase.
Two primary personas:
Traveler — A returning student, family visitor, or business traveler with confirmed flight tickets and 5-25 kg of unused checked allowance. Motivated by side income, typically MYR 200-800 per trip.
Sender — Diaspora workers in Malaysia sending gifts, documents, electronics, or specialty foods to family in Bangladesh, or small e-commerce operators sourcing from KL markets. Motivated by lower cost and/or faster delivery than DHL/FedEx.
1.3 Headline User Flow — Traveler
The traveler-side flow is structured to convert intent (a confirmed flight) into a monetized listing in under five minutes, while gating actual transactional capability behind verification.
Sign-up & onboarding — Email/password or Google Sign-In. The user lands on a four-screen onboarding carousel (the dynamic video portal) covering how the platform works, the bidding flow, prohibited goods, and safety protocols.
KYC submission — User uploads government ID front, ID back, and a live selfie. Submission moves to the moderation queue. The user can browse the platform but cannot post or accept until approved.
Profile completion — User adds payout details, optional spoken languages, and trip history if any. Verified profile gets a green badge.
Flight posting — User fills the flight form: origin airport, destination airport, departure datetime, arrival datetime, airline, flight number, available kilograms, accepted item categories, asking price per kg or fixed total. Posting deducts a fixed flight_post_cost in points. The flight enters DRAFT until points are deducted, then becomes LIVE.
Bid management — Senders submit bids against the listing. The traveler sees ranked bids, accepts one, counters, or declines. Acceptance moves the bid to AGREED.
Pre-flight handoff — Sender and traveler exchange contact via in-app channel only after AGREED. Sender hands over packed items at an agreed location with a documented item manifest and photos. Traveler signs a digital handoff receipt.
In-flight & delivery — Traveler marks status milestones: PICKED_UP, DEPARTED, ARRIVED, DELIVERED. Delivery requires a recipient signature/photo and a confirmation code shared with the sender.
Settlement — Once both parties confirm DELIVERED with no dispute window violation, traveler payout is released. A 1-5 star rating is exchanged.
1.4 Headline User Flow — Sender
Sign-up & KYC — Same gate as traveler. A sender cannot place a bid without an approved KYC.
Discovery — Search by destination, departure window, minimum kg, urgency. Results sort by relevance, then by traveler rating, then by price. An urgency boost surfaces senders' requests but here it is the traveler's listings being browsed.
Listing detail — Sender views traveler profile, rating, completed trips, accepted categories, route, and price. A trust panel shows verification badges.
Bidding — Sender submits a bid: weight in kg, item category, declared item value, special handling notes, optional urgency boost (extra points for top-of-list placement on the traveler's bid view). Each bid submission costs a points fee.
Bid lifecycle — Bid is PENDING until traveler responds. If accepted, it becomes AGREED, both parties exchange contact, and a transaction record opens.
Handoff — Sender prepares items per platform rules (no prohibited goods, item manifest, photos). Sender confirms HANDED_OVER in-app.
Tracking — Sender follows status updates posted by the traveler.
Receipt & rating — Recipient confirms with a one-time code. Sender rates the traveler. Funds release.

2. The Points / Connects Economy
2.1 Design Philosophy
The points economy serves three jobs and only three jobs. It must rate-limit low-quality activity (spam listings, unserious bids), it must allow the platform to monetize before transaction-level revenue is mature, and it must give power users a controllable lever (urgency boost) without breaking fairness for everyone else. Anything beyond these three jobs is feature creep and should be rejected during reviews.
The model deliberately follows Upwork's Connects pattern rather than an ad-auction model because two-sided marketplaces with low transaction frequency per user (a sender ships maybe 2-4 times a year) cannot sustain a true bidding marketplace for visibility without crushing organic discovery. Connects-style economies create just enough friction to filter, without becoming pay-to-win.

2.5 Urgency Mechanics — Detailed Logic
Urgency is the single most abusable lever in the system, so the rules need to be unambiguous and server-enforced. The implementation rests on a small number of invariants.
Urgency boosts are time-bound, not action-bound. A 24-hour boost expires on a wall-clock timer regardless of how many travelers see the bid.
Boosts apply only to bids in PENDING state. The moment a bid moves to AGREED, DECLINED, or EXPIRED, any active boost is auto-cancelled and pro-rata points are not refunded.
A sender may have at most one active level-3 (Express) boost per 24-hour period across all bids. This prevents spamming all listings on a popular route with Express boosts.
Level-3 boosts trigger an admin-side notification only on routes flagged as PRIORITY_CORRIDORS in the platform config. Admins manually reach out only during the soft-launch period; this transitions to fully automated push notifications once the carrier base hits a threshold.
Boosts decay in ranking weight. A bid with a boost applied 22 hours ago ranks below an identical bid with a boost applied 1 hour ago, even at the same level. This forces senders to apply urgency genuinely close to their deadline.
2.6 Anti-Abuse Rules
Six rules every points operation must satisfy before it commits:
All point mutations write to the points_ledger collection in the same Firestore transaction as the balance update. Never update balance without writing a ledger entry.
Refunds are themselves ledger entries with a refund_of pointer to the original entry. Never adjust an old entry.
Hard cap on monthly free points accumulation (60 pts). Beyond cap, the monthly bonus is silently dropped, not stockpiled.
Points purchased via card are non-refundable in points but refundable in MYR via the dispute process. This prevents purchase-then-refund laundering.
Daily velocity check: any user spending more than 500 pts in 24 hours is flagged for admin review on the next event. Not blocked, just flagged.
Promotional points (sign-up, monthly, referral) cannot be used for direct purchase refunds and are tracked in a separate balance field for accounting separation.

3. Database Schema — Firestore
3.1 Schema Design Principles
Firestore is a document database with collection-level indexes. A few rules govern good schema design here. Keep documents small (under 100 KB ceiling, ideally under 5 KB hot path). Denormalize aggressively for read performance, accept the write-fanout cost, and use Cloud Functions to keep denormalized fields in sync. Subcollections beat nested arrays for any list that can grow unboundedly. Composite indexes are the bottleneck on cost and latency, so the schema below pre-declares the indexes the access patterns require.
The platform uses thirteen top-level collections. Five subcollections are scoped to user or flight documents. Every collection has its own security rules, ownership semantics, and write surface.

4. Technical Implementation Strategy
4.1 High-Level Architecture
SpareKG is a single Next.js application (App Router, server components where useful) backed by Firebase services. The architecture deliberately avoids a separate Node/Express backend in v1: Cloud Functions for Firebase serve as the trusted compute layer, and Firestore security rules serve as the authorization layer. This keeps the operational footprint small enough for a four-person team to manage without dedicated DevOps.
The boundary between client-trusted writes and server-only writes is the most important architectural decision in the system. All financially-relevant mutations (point deductions, status transitions, payout releases) go through Cloud Functions. The client never writes to points_ledger, transactions, or audit_log directly. Firestore security rules enforce this with deny-all on those collections except for service-account writes.
4.2 Repository Layout
A monorepo is overkill at this scale. A flat Next.js app with a typed Firebase Admin SDK in functions/ is sufficient. Suggested layout:

4.3 RTK Query + Firebase Integration Pattern
RTK Query does not have a first-party Firebase fetcher, but it pairs cleanly via a custom baseQuery. The pattern is to bypass HTTP entirely and treat each endpoint as a function that calls the Firebase JS SDK directly. This gives the developer ergonomics of RTK Query (caching, invalidation, hooks, optimistic updates) with the realtime sync of Firestore as a separate concern handled outside RTK.
Reference implementation for the baseQuery:
// features/baseQuery.ts
import { BaseQueryFn } from '@reduxjs/toolkit/query';
import { FirebaseError } from 'firebase/app';

type FirebaseQueryArg = {
  invoke: () => Promise<unknown>;
};

export const firebaseBaseQuery: BaseQueryFn<FirebaseQueryArg> =
  async ({ invoke }) => {
    try {
      const data = await invoke();
      return { data };
    } catch (err) {
      const e = err as FirebaseError;
      return { error: { code: e.code, message: e.message } };
    }
  };
Each feature defines its endpoints by composing Firebase SDK calls inside invoke. Tag-based cache invalidation works exactly as it does for HTTP endpoints. For realtime collections (the user's bid feed, active transaction list), use Firestore onSnapshot listeners that dispatch updateQueryData to keep RTK Query's cache in sync without polling. This hybrid pattern — RTK Query for command-style reads/writes plus onSnapshot for hot lists — is the cleanest one we have found at this scale.
4.4 Authentication Flow
Firebase Authentication is the identity provider. Sign-up and sign-in support email/password and Google Sign-In. Phone OTP is deliberately out of scope for v1 due to Firebase's per-project SMS quotas and the cost of bulk SMS in Bangladesh; we collect phone post-sign-up for handoff coordination but do not use it as a primary auth factor.
Token handling: client stores ID tokens via the Firebase SDK's persistence layer. For Next.js server components and API routes that need authenticated server work, we forward the ID token in an HTTP-only cookie set after sign-in, then verify it server-side via the Admin SDK's verifyIdToken. Custom claims (admin: true, kycApproved: true) are set by Cloud Functions on KYC approval and propagate to clients on next token refresh (one-hour cycle, or via forceRefresh after critical state changes).
4.5 Firestore Security Rules — Skeleton
Security rules are the load-bearing wall of this architecture. The skeleton below illustrates the layering. Real rules expand each function with field-level checks.
rules_version = '2';
service cloud.firestore {
  match /databases/{database}/documents {

    function isSignedIn() { return request.auth != null; }
    function isVerified() {
      return isSignedIn() && request.auth.token.kycApproved == true;
    }
    function isAdmin() {
      return isSignedIn() && request.auth.token.admin == true;
    }
    function isOwner(uid) {
      return isSignedIn() && request.auth.uid == uid;
    }

    match /users/{userId} {
      allow read: if isSignedIn();
      allow create: if isOwner(userId);
      allow update: if isOwner(userId)
        && !('pointsBalance' in request.resource.data.diff(resource.data).affectedKeys())
        && !('roles' in request.resource.data.diff(resource.data).affectedKeys());
      allow delete: if false;
    }

    match /flights/{flightId} {
      allow read: if isSignedIn();
      allow create: if isVerified()
        && request.resource.data.travelerId == request.auth.uid;
      allow update: if isVerified()
        && resource.data.travelerId == request.auth.uid
        && resource.data.status in ['DRAFT', 'LIVE'];
      allow delete: if isAdmin();
    }

    match /points_ledger/{entryId} { allow read: if isOwner(resource.data.userId) || isAdmin(); allow write: if false; }
    match /transactions/{txId} { allow read: if isOwner(resource.data.travelerId) || isOwner(resource.data.senderId) || isAdmin(); allow write: if false; }
    match /audit_log/{entryId} { allow read: if isAdmin(); allow write: if false; }
  }
}

4.10 Mobile Strategy

A pure React Native rewrite is rejected for v1 because the cost of maintaining two UI codebases with a four-person team outweighs the marginal native-feel gain.

5. Security & Trust
5.1 KYC — The Trust Foundation
Every safety mechanism in the platform — the dispute process, the points ledger audit, the user reports queue — collapses if KYC is weak. The KYC pipeline must be treated as a critical-path security system, not an onboarding inconvenience.
5.1.1 Document Capture
Required artifacts at minimum: government photo ID front, ID back where applicable, and a live selfie captured in-app. The selfie must be captured through the platform's camera flow, not uploaded from the gallery. This is enforced by using the MediaStream API directly and rejecting file inputs on the selfie step. A short liveness prompt (turn head left, then right) raises the bar against static-photo spoofing without requiring a paid third-party liveness vendor in v1.
5.1.2 Storage & Access
ID images and selfies are stored in a Cloud Storage bucket with a security rule that allows write only by the owning user (path-prefixed by UID) and read only by service accounts. The Cloud Function that surfaces images to the admin moderation UI generates short-lived signed URLs, never raw storage URLs. ID number fields are encrypted at rest using a Cloud KMS-managed key; the decryption operation is logged to audit_log and tied to a moderation review event.
5.1.3 Moderation Queue
Submissions enter the moderation queue in FIFO order with a service-level target of 12 hours from submission to first-pass decision. Admins may approve, reject with a categorized reason (drives a templated user-facing message), or escalate. Re-submissions after rejection are allowed with a 1-hour cooldown to prevent spam. After three rejections the account is auto-flagged for senior-admin review.
Phase-2 enhancement: integrate a third-party identity verifier (Veriff, Onfido, or Sumsub) as a first-pass filter so admins only see edge cases. This is deferred until volume justifies the per-check cost (typically USD 0.50-2.00 per submission).
5.2 Trust Signals — Surfacing Safety to Users
Verification status alone is not enough. The platform must make trust legible at every decision moment.
Verified badge — Green check on profile photo, on every flight card, and on every bid card. Always visible.
Trip count — '24 successful trips' as a sub-label on traveler profiles, derived from completedTripsAsTraveler.
Average rating — Stars + count, never just stars. '4.8 (32)' is more trustworthy than just '4.8'.
Cancellation rate — Visible to other party only after AGREED. Hidden in browse view to avoid pre-judgment based on small samples.
Time-on-platform — 'Member since March 2026'. Discourages new throwaway accounts.
ID country match — On the bid acceptance screen, surface whether the traveler's KYC country matches a route the bidder is sending to. Soft signal, not a block.
5.3 Prohibited Goods Policy
Prohibited goods are the single largest legal liability in this business. The policy must be unambiguous, enforced both at the user-education layer and the bid-validation layer, and revisable as customs regulations on either corridor change.
5.3.1 Hard-Prohibited Categories (server-rejected)
Currency in any form, gold bars or unmounted gold, jewellery above declared value threshold.
Firearms, ammunition, weapons of any classification.
Narcotics, controlled substances, prescription medication for parties other than the traveler.
Live animals, animal parts, or perishable biological material.
Lithium batteries above 100 Wh, loose lithium cells, e-cigarettes with batteries shipped without device.
Liquids above the destination customs threshold, alcohol where destination customs prohibits.
Counterfeit goods, replicas of luxury goods, anything bearing a trademark not authorized for resale.
Documents that constitute legal instruments — passports, visas, birth certificates of third parties, court documents.
5.3.2 Soft-Restricted Categories (require traveler explicit accept)
Electronics with non-removable batteries (laptops, phones in original packaging).
Cosmetics and personal care items (volume thresholds apply).
Powdered foods and spices (often customs-flagged in Bangladesh).
Books and printed materials (some titles are restricted at destination).
Religious or ceremonial items.
5.3.3 Enforcement Mechanics
Item-category dropdown is canonical and bound to the policy version. New bids cannot select free-text categories.
Hard-prohibited categories are not even available in the dropdown. They are listed in the help text with explanation.
Soft-restricted categories trigger an in-app modal with policy summary and a tick-box that the traveler must accept on bid acceptance.
Reports of prohibited goods bypass the normal queue and route directly to the senior-admin review track.
Confirmed violations result in immediate account suspension, points forfeiture, and (depending on jurisdiction) reporting to authorities.
5.4 Off-Platform Contact Risk
Users will try to take agreed deals off-platform to avoid platform fees. This is bad for revenue and worse for safety, because off-platform deals lose all dispute protection. Mitigations:
Contact details (phone, address) are revealed only after a bid reaches AGREED state, never before.
In-app chat is the primary communication channel. Numeric strings that look like phone numbers or email addresses in pre-AGREED chat are masked client-side and flagged server-side.
A user reporting an off-platform contact attempt with evidence triggers admin review. Confirmed cases result in suspension and points forfeiture.
Keep platform fees moderate enough that off-platform circumvention isn't worth the dispute-protection loss. The 8-12% transaction fee target is calibrated for this.
5.5 Dispute Process
Disputes are inevitable. The architecture provides the rails for resolving them within the platform rather than escalating to the courts.
Dispute window opens at HANDED_OVER and closes 72 hours after DELIVERED.
Either party may open a dispute. Opening freezes the payout in escrow.
Both parties submit evidence (photos, chat transcripts, manifests) within 48 hours of dispute opening.
Admin reviews against a defined rubric. Decisions: full payout to traveler, full refund to sender, or split. Admin notes are mandatory and auditable.
First dispute against a user is informational. Three confirmed disputes within 90 days triggers automatic suspension pending review.
5.6 Insurance & Liability — A Note for v1
v1 ships without insurance. The Terms of Service must clearly cap platform liability at the platform fee paid for the disputed transaction. Declared item value on bids exists for transparency, not coverage. v2 considerations: per-transaction insurance via partner (typical premium 1-3% of declared value), or self-insured pool funded by a small slice of platform fees.

6. Admin Dashboard & Super-Admin Capabilities
6.1 Architecture & Access Control
The admin panel is a route-guarded section of the same Next.js application, mounted at /admin. It is not a separate codebase. This decision trades a small amount of bundle size for a large amount of operational simplicity: the admin team uses the same auth, the same Firestore SDK, the same component library, and ships in the same release cadence.
Access is gated by the admin Firebase custom claim. Setting this claim is itself a privileged operation, performed only by a callable Cloud Function whose access list of super-admin UIDs is hardcoded in the function's code (not in Firestore, to prevent privilege escalation via a Firestore breach). The function logs every grant or revoke to audit_log.
Two role tiers in v1:
Admin — KYC moderation, dispute resolution, content management, user suspension, points adjustment up to a daily cap.
Super-Admin — All admin powers plus role grants, app_config edits, financial dashboards, unlimited points adjustment, account deletions.
6.2 Module: KYC Moderation Queue
The single highest-leverage admin tool. SLA target: 12 hours from submission to decision.
6.2.1 Features
FIFO queue view with filterable status (PENDING, UNDER_REVIEW, REJECTED for re-review).
Claim/release semantics — admin claiming a submission moves it to UNDER_REVIEW with their UID. Prevents duplicate work in a multi-admin setting.
Side-by-side image viewer — ID front, ID back, selfie, with zoom and rotate. Signed URLs auto-refresh every 15 minutes.
Cross-check panel — extracted name and DOB from ID alongside user-submitted name and DOB, with mismatch highlighting.
Decision dial — Approve, Reject (with templated reason dropdown), Escalate (route to super-admin), Request Resubmission (sends user-facing message).
Reject reasons are an enum with templated user-facing messages in EN and BN. No free-text rejection messages — this prevents inconsistent communication and reduces support load.
Bulk actions — only allowed for clearly-bot submissions. Default UI is single-decision to enforce attention.
6.2.2 Data Model
The queue is backed by a Firestore query against kyc_submissions where status is PENDING or UNDER_REVIEW, sorted by submittedAt ascending. With composite index on (status, submittedAt) this is O(1) on page size. Decisions write back to the submission, update the user doc's kycStatus field, and trigger the onKycApproved Cloud Function on approve.
6.3 Module: Economy Oversight
The points economy is a virtual currency. The risk surface is the same as any currency system: inflation (over-issuance from buggy promo logic), fraud (chargebacks on point purchases), and abuse (collusion rings inflating completed-trip rewards). The economy oversight module exists to catch all three classes early.
6.3.1 Real-Time Economy Dashboard
Single page, refreshes on a 60-second polling cycle. Backed by a small set of pre-aggregated counters maintained by Cloud Functions on points_ledger writes. Direct queries against the full ledger are reserved for the audit view, not the dashboard.
Total points outstanding — sum of all user pointsBalance fields. Should equal cumulative_credits − cumulative_debits.
Last 24h: points issued (split by category), points spent (split by category), points purchased (in MYR and points).
Last 7d trend chart — issued vs. spent vs. purchased, line chart.
Top 20 spenders and top 20 earners (last 7 days). Click-through to user profile.
Reconciliation indicator — green if outstanding equals credits−debits within rounding, red if drift detected.
6.3.2 Ledger Audit View
Searchable, filterable view of the full points_ledger. Filters: user, category, reference type, date range, delta sign.
Export to CSV for accounting.
Drill-down: clicking a ledger entry opens the source record (flight, bid, transaction, purchase).
Reverse-entry tool — super-admin can issue an adjustment entry that nets a problematic prior entry to zero. Original entry is never modified. The adjustment is itself logged with the admin's UID and a mandatory reason.
6.3.3 Abuse Heuristics
A nightly scheduled function flags suspicious patterns, surfacing them in an admin review queue:
Users earning more than 200 pts per day for three consecutive days from REFERRAL or TRIP_REWARD categories — possible referral ring or self-trip collusion.
Pairs of users transacting more than three times within seven days — possible round-trip laundering.
Users spending purchased points exclusively on bids that immediately become DELIVERED with same recipient phone number — possible self-shipment.
Sudden spikes in monthly free points claims from new accounts created within 24 hours — possible bot wave.
6.4 Module: Platform Analytics
Distinct from the economy dashboard. Where economy oversight is about correctness, analytics is about growth and operations. Refresh cadence here can be slower (hourly) and the source can be a BigQuery export of Firestore for richer queries; v1 gets by with on-demand aggregation queries since data volume is low.
6.4.1 Top-Level KPI Strip
Daily active users (DAU) — split by primary role of session (Traveler-DAU vs Sender-DAU vs both).
Weekly active users (WAU) and monthly active users (MAU).
DAU/MAU ratio — engagement health metric.
Total flights posted today, this week, this month.
Total bids submitted, total bids accepted, ratio (= match rate).
Total transaction volume in MYR (gross merchandise value).
Platform revenue (sum of platform fees on settled transactions).
6.4.2 Route Heatmap
A visual list of corridors ranked by transaction volume in the last 30 days. Each corridor shows: number of LIVE flights, number of pending bids, average price per kg, average time-to-acceptance. This tells the team where to focus growth — adding a corridor is meaningless if the existing ones are under-supplied.
6.4.3 Funnel Views
Acquisition funnel — sign-up → KYC submitted → KYC approved → first action (post or bid) → first transaction. Conversion at each step, with cohort splits by acquisition source.
Bid funnel — flight viewed → flight detail viewed → bid composed → bid submitted → bid accepted → handoff confirmed → delivery confirmed. Drop-off identification.
Retention curve — cohort-week retention by sign-up week. Targets 30%+ at week 4.
6.4.4 Operational Health
KYC queue depth and median age (alarm if >24 hours).
Dispute queue depth and median age.
Failed payment rate.
Cloud Function error rate by function name.
Firestore read/write trends with cost projection.
6.5 Module: Dispute & Content Management
6.5.1 Dispute Console
Dispute queue with status filters (OPEN, UNDER_REVIEW, awaiting evidence, RESOLVED).
Single dispute view: full transaction context, both parties' submissions, evidence files, chat transcript inline.
Decision actions: payout to traveler, refund to sender, split (with editable percentages), close as invalid.
Mandatory rationale field — minimum 50 characters. Shown to both parties in their notification.
Strike system — every confirmed dispute against a user is recorded; thresholds trigger automatic actions per 5.5.
6.5.2 User Management
Search by email, phone, UID, displayName.
Profile detail view with full activity timeline (flights, bids, transactions, points, reports filed against, reports filed by).
Suspend/unsuspend with mandatory reason and end-date (or indefinite). Suspension flips the suspended flag, which Firestore rules read on every write.
Force KYC re-submission — moves user back to PENDING, requires fresh documents.
Account deletion (super-admin only) — soft-delete that anonymizes PII while preserving ledger and audit history.
6.5.3 Reports Triage
Inbox of user-submitted reports, sorted by severity (prohibited goods > harassment > off-platform attempt > other).
Each report shows reporter, reported entity (user, flight, bid, message), and free-text claim.
Quick actions: dismiss, suspend reported user, forward to dispute console, escalate to super-admin.
6.5.4 Content Management
This is where the dynamic onboarding video portal and other public-facing content is managed.
Onboarding videos — list of slides, each with order, EN/BN titles and descriptions, video file (Cloud Storage upload), thumbnail, active flag. Drag-and-drop reorder. Preview rendering identical to user-facing slider.
Marketing banners and announcement bars — schedule start/end times, link targets, show only to specific roles.
FAQ entries — question, answer (markdown), category, EN/BN, ordered.
Prohibited goods policy versions — semantically versioned. Past versions are retained for legal traceability of what policy was in effect at time of any historical bid.
App config — fee schedule, points pricing tiers, monthly free amount, urgency boost costs, KYC required reason templates. Changes require super-admin and emit an audit_log entry.
6.6 Audit Trail
Every admin action — KYC decision, dispute resolution, points adjustment, suspension, content edit, role grant — writes an entry to audit_log. The entry captures: actorUid, action, targetType, targetId, beforeState (where applicable), afterState, reason, timestamp, IP address, user-agent. The audit_log collection is read-only for admins, write-only for service accounts. Super-admins can query but not edit. This collection is what protects the platform if a bad actor reaches admin role and what protects admins if their actions are challenged.
6.7 Admin UI Patterns — Implementation Notes
Use the same component library as the consumer app, but with a denser layout — admin work involves processing many records quickly, so favor table density over whitespace.
Keyboard shortcuts on KYC and dispute queues — J/K for next/prev, A for approve, R for reject. Reduces moderation time meaningfully.
All long-running admin actions (bulk operations, exports) run as Cloud Functions and report status back via a notifications channel; the admin UI is never blocked on a long operation.
Admin sessions have shorter idle timeouts (30 minutes) than consumer sessions.
Two-factor authentication is mandatory for any account with admin or super-admin claim, enforced at sign-in by checking the auth token's MFA factor list.
